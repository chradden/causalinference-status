Exploratory Data Analysis — EIA RECS 2020¶

Project: Beyond Correlation in Energy Data — the heterogeneous causal effect of energy-efficient windows on residential energy intensity.

Data: U.S. Energy Information Administration, Residential Energy Consumption Survey 2020. 17,196 U.S. households after filtering; processed by ../data/load_recs.py. Real, nationally representative survey data — public domain.

This notebook covers: dataset overview, missing values, feature distributions, correlations, possible biases, and the confounding pattern that motivates the causal approach.

Dataset overview & target variable¶

  • Treatment T — efficient_windows (binary): 1 = double/triple-pane, 0 = single-pane. Recoded from RECS TYPEGLASS. Window replacement is a canonical energy-efficiency retrofit.
  • Outcome Y — energy_intensity_kwh_m2 (continuous): annual household energy use (all fuels) per m² of conditioned floor area. Derived from TOTALBTU / TOTSQFT_EN.
  • Covariates X: floor area, building age, climate zone, housing type, household size, income bin.

The confounding picture — why naive comparison misleads¶

Households with efficient windows are not a random sample. They tend to live in newer / smaller homes, in different climate zones, with different incomes. So a raw comparison of T=1 vs T=0 mixes the true causal effect of efficient windows with all these differences.

Insights¶

  • Energy intensity is right-skewed with a national mean ≈ 150 kWh/m²·a, consistent with U.S. residential benchmarks.
  • Cold/Very-Cold climate zones have the highest consumption.
  • The naive treatment contrast is ≈ −12 kWh/m²·a (efficient-window homes use less). But:
  • The probability of having efficient windows is strongly confounded by climate and building age: newer / cold-climate homes have efficient windows much more often.
  • → A naive comparison would attribute the climate/age effect to the window upgrade. We need to adjust for confounders (Baseline OLS in step 2, Causal Forest in step 3).
